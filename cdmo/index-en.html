Provenance of this page
Ontology Specification Draft
CDMO is a domain-independent (upper/mid-level) ontology that models the generic structure of decision-making in complex, safety-critical environments. It is designed as a schema (TBox) to ground a large language model, so that the model emits an instance graph (ABox) that is SHACL-validated, queryable, and auditable. CDMO is deliberately empty of domain facts and is specialised per domain. It reuses the PROV-O and OWL-Time ontologies.
CDMO organises the anatomy of a decision episode into nine interlinked conceptual modules: Situation & Context — the situation, its environment, entities, states, events, observations, and the perception / comprehension / projection levels of situation assessment. Agents & Roles — human, AI and organizational agents; roles, decision authority, capabilities and stakeholders. Goals, Constraints & Values — objectives, goals, hard/soft constraints, values and success criteria. Decision Core — decision problems and points, courses of action, actions, plans and decisions. Reasoning, Evidence & Argument — Toulmin-aligned claims, evidence, arguments, warrants, backing, rebuttals, qualifiers, assumptions and rationale. Uncertainty & Risk — uncertainty, confidence, risk, threats, hazards, vulnerabilities and consequences. Evaluation & Outcome — criteria, trade-offs, evaluations, outcomes and feedback. Causality & Time — causal relations, preconditions and effects, anchored on OWL-Time. Provenance & Trust — sources, reliability and trustworthiness, grounded on PROV-O. The schema was exercised across three safety-critical domains — emergency medicine, border security and disaster management — to evaluate whether knowledge-graph grounding improves the auditability and consistency of an LLM's decision support.
The ontology was developed following the HCOME human-centered, collaborative ontology engineering methodology, with conceptualisation carried out in a personal space (e.g. Protégé) and consolidated in a shared space through argumentation and consensus. The methodology was extended along the human-to-LLM-centered direction (SimX-HCOME+), in which large language models act as co-engineering agents that play knowledge-worker personas (domain expert, engineer, critic), while human experts retain ratification authority. The involved team combined ontology engineers with domain experts from three safety-critical domains — emergency medicine, border security and disaster management — who supplied operational scenarios, validated the conceptualisation, and served as the human expert baseline against which LLM-generated graphs were scored. Following the HCOME principle of reuse before reinvention, CDMO reuses PROV-O for the provenance & trust module and the OWL-Time ontology for the causality & time module, and uses SKOS, Dublin Core Terms and VANN for term annotation and metadata. A full requirements specification (ORSD) and competency questions accompany the ontology in METHODOLOGY.md.
